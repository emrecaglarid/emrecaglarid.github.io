World Building for Designers

PhD research on making the speculative process legible: a theoretical model and open-source toolset for designing in future contexts.

Role
Researcher
Year
2015–2021
Scope
PhD · Action research · Tool design · Design education
Institution
METU Industrial Design

Design students working on future contexts have a problem that doesn't get talked about much: they can't do field research into places that don't exist yet. When a student is designing for rural Africa in 2040, or for a world where organ tissue grows on demand, or for any context they've never experienced, they fill the gap with a combination of research, imagination, analogy, and personal conviction. Most of that process is undocumented and unstructured.

My PhD thesis was about making that process legible.

The Research Question

The fuzzy front-end of design, the chaotic early phase before solutions are on the table, is where some of the most consequential decisions happen. What context are we designing for? What assumptions are we embedding into it? How do those assumptions hold up when tested against reality?

When the context is the future, designers often can't validate assumptions through conventional research. They build fictional worlds instead: detailed, internally consistent versions of a future context that can hold a design brief. This is world building, a concept borrowed from media studies, where it refers to the process of constructing complete, plausible fictional worlds that serve as the setting for a story.

My observation was that design students were doing this already. They were just doing it without a framework, without shared vocabulary, and without reliable ways to assess whether the worlds they were building were coherent or viable. The research questions were: can we describe this process in a theoretical model, and can we give designers tools to do it better?

How I Approached It

The method was action research: design a toolset, use it in workshops with design students, analyze the results, and iterate. Over five years at METU, I ran three workshops.

The first two I organized myself, controlled settings that gave me space to test early versions of the toolset and observe how students built worlds. The third was embedded inside an actual fourth-year studio course, which let me observe the toolset operating inside a real design project over a longer period.

The iterative design of the toolset wasn't just a methodology convenience. It produced the theoretical model. Each workshop revealed something about how world building actually works: what students do when they speculate, how they assess their own speculations, what makes one fictional world feel believable and another feel like a list of arbitrary ideas. The model was built from observing those patterns across three workshops.

What I Found

The theoretical model describes world building in design as a process with three interconnected phases: speculation, assessment, and centering.

Speculation builds the fictional world. Assessment tests it against pragmatic concerns. Centering is where the fictional world and the real world meet and the design brief emerges. The process isn't linear: students move between speculation and assessment repeatedly throughout a project. The model represents this as a wave rather than a sequence.

Speculation

Speculation is the phase where students build a textual reference world, a fictional version of the future context constructed from a mix of real research and imagined extensions of it. The smallest units of this world are what I called props, borrowed from philosophy of fiction. Props can be metaphorical (an analogy from the real world mapped into the future context) or causal (a consequence that follows logically from something already established in the world). Metaphorical props tend to establish the initial arcs of a world; causal props develop them further.

Assessment

Assessment is when students test their fictional world against pragmatic concerns: how technologically plausible is this? How optimistic or pessimistic does the team feel about it? Does it make sense given what we know today? I identified five dimensions along which students assessed their speculations in practice: social, emotional, technological, temporal, and scope-based. The peer evaluation data from the second workshop showed that worlds with more causally developed, internally consistent arcs were rated more believable by other students.

Centering

Centering is the blending phase, where the fictional world and the actual world meet and the design brief emerges. Students don't simply choose the fictional world over the real one, or vice versa; they find a point where the two are reconciled. The design solution lives in that space.

The Toolset

The World Building for Designers (WBD) toolset was the practical output of the research. It operationalizes the theoretical model into a structured workshop format:

  • A research phase for sorting findings into informational (factual, grounding) and inspirational (imaginative, triggering)
  • A kickstart phase of timed descriptive exercises to establish early world building
  • A speculating phase of open-ended prototyping exercises
  • An assessment phase using arc sheets and structured reflection prompts
  • A detailing phase where design solutions are generated and placed back into the world

The toolset was designed for use both in physical workshops (printable cards and sheets) and digitally (a Miro template). It was open-sourced under GNU GPL v3 and made available on GitHub.

After the dissertation was submitted, the toolset was taken up by two other institutions without prompting, the closest thing to a real-world adoption metric the research produced.

What It Connects To

I'd been a teaching assistant in the Industrial Design department throughout the research, running studios and watching how students built their contexts. That position gave me access to the workshops and shaped my research questions from the start.

The experience of designing tools for designers, building something, putting it in front of people, watching where it breaks down, and iterating, was formative. Most of what I care about in product design now has roots in this: that the processes people use to make sense of unfamiliar things are worth studying carefully, that good tools make implicit processes explicit without removing the ambiguity that makes them useful, and that the gap between what designers say they do and what they actually do is where the interesting problems are.
